German Pronunciation Guide for English Speakers

Standard German has relatively steady vowel sounds and some consonant patterns that differ from English. This guide uses IPA symbols and compares German sounds to approximate English equivalents. Note that regional accents (Austrian, Swiss, Bavarian, etc.) may differ.

a /a/ Mann [man] Like "a" in "father," but shorter
e (short) /ɛ/ Bett [bɛt] Like "e" in "bed"
e (long) /eː/ lesen [ˈleː.zən] Like "ay" in "say," but without glide
i /iː/ Liebe [ˈliː.bə] Like "ee" in "machine"
o /oː/ Brot [broːt] Similar to "o" in "go," but pure and without glide
u /uː/ gut [ɡuːt] Like "oo" in "food"

ch /ç/ or /x/ ich [ɪç], Bach [bax] /ç/ like hissing "h" in "hue"; /x/ like Scottish "loch"

Stress and Vowel Length

German stress usually falls on the first syllable of the word. Vowel length is important: long and short vowels change meaning (Stadt [ʃtat] = "city" vs Staat [ʃtaːt] = "state").

Varieties of Pronunciation

Standard German: used in most teaching materials and broadcast media.
Regional Accents: Southern German, Swiss, and Austrian varieties may pronounce r, vowels, and diphthongs differently.
Colloquial Speech: final consonants may be devoiced (e.g., "ab" pronounced [ap]).

Helpful Symbols and Conventions

IPA brackets [ ] show exact sounds; slashes / / show phonemes. Umlauts (ä, ö, ü) represent fronted vowels not found in English. Double consonants mean the preceding vowel is short (Mitte [ˈmɪ.tə]).
